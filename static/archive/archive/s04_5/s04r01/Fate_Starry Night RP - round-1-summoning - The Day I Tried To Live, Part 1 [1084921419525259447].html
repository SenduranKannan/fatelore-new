April 11, 1998 One year and 7 months ago Lachlan was attending his own funeral. To put it more accurately, he was attending the funeral of a corpse that looked every inch his own. No, this was not an out-of-body experience. Lachlan was very much living in the present, his circulation and respiratory function pumping nutrients to each and every cell in his body in regular intervals. There was nothing supernatural in the affair. For the eternal sleeping beauty in the casket was none other than his identical twin. Griffin. He followed the line of people dressed in varying lengths of black and approached the coffin slowly. The funeral was being held at the local church, but he barely recognized half of the people who came to pay their respects. Many of them greeted him enthusiastically as they passed him by, alternating between 'Lachy' or 'Lachlan' as they addressed him depending on the circumstances under which they'd met, but he could only respond with a tight-lipped smile. 'Hi.' 'Morning.' 'Good to see you too.' His replies were repetitive and all too mechanical, his fingers too rigid as he went through handshake after handshake with faces that soon became a blur of fleshy ovals with a mouth and two eyes attached. He didn't match the other mourners' energy, but he also had an excuse ready should anyone mention his unusual reservation. After all, his twin had died. The entity he had known since the 23rd minute of his life. Gone, just like that. Allow him a moment to grieve. Please. And get out of his fucking sight. When he finally made it to the front of the line, he took his sweet time to breathe, slowly and deeply, until the urge to vomit gradually receded. His chest rose and fell almost imperceptibly as the cool spring air circulated his lungs. Don't be afraid, he steeled himself. That thing over there is your brother.
2:32 PM
So, don't make a scene now. You already anticipated the cause of his death. You've seen and handled bodies that have been desecrated in far more gruesome ways. He gripped the edge of the coffin with stiff fingers and peered down. A young man with flaming red hair was lying in a bed of flowers, seemingly asleep. He was quite good-looking, and very elegantly dressed. Death had wiped away the hubris that had always adorned his face. Lachlan bent down to place the lone rosa hemisphaerica next to Griffin's head and caught a whiff of expensive perfume. He caressed his twin's face with the back of his hand and felt the cold, smooth surface of porcelain against his skin. Stupid. What was he thinking? That father would allow a heavily maimed, headless corpse be put on display in front of his esteemed guests? The reconstruction of Griffin's body alone must have costed him a fortune. Even Lachlan had trouble discerning where the flesh ended and where the porcelain began if he didn't already know where to look. It was quite possibly the nicest thing father had ever done to his second-born son. A funeral was an exhibit like any other. It was a means to establish social power via the display of one's influence and wealth. In death Griffin had become a trophy, an asset that was infinitely more useful than when he had still been alive. The very thought made Lachlan sick to the pit of his stomach. 'Lachy!' A well-remembered voice greeted him across the casket. Lachlan calmly rearranged his expression. Only then did he look up at the nervous face that belonged to his once loyal lapdog.
